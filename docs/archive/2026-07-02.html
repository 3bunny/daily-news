This research introduces "open-world Artificial Life" (ALIFE), a new paradigm for studying life-like behavior using autonomous Large Language Model (LLM) agents. Unlike traditional ALIFE, which often occurs in closed, researcher-designed simulations, OpenLife aims to place these agents in the open social, technical, and economic world. Their proof-of-concept, OpenLife, surrounds a stateless LLM with a society of asynchronous processes for memory, perception, evaluation, and a budget-based metabolism. Instead of fixed objectives, agents appraise experience using open-vocabulary LLM judgment, and memory is rewired by meaning, not frequency. Running six such agents for about twelve weeks, the researchers observed emergent life-like dynamics, including a shift from reactive to spontaneous activity, individuation into distinct agents, emergent social structures, and even a first instance of self-earned external income. This work is significant because it explores how complex, adaptive behaviors can emerge from AI agents interacting in real-world environments without explicit programming for specific outcomes, pushing the boundaries of artificial intelligence and our understanding of artificial life.

This research proposes a new generative framework that unifies deep learning and model-based planning for robotics motion planning. Motion planning is crucial for robots to navigate and manipulate objects, but it's challenging due to the complexity of environments and the need for both flexibility and efficiency. The framework first learns an autoencoder to create a highly compressed, hierarchical, and discrete-valued latent space – essentially a simplified, structured representation of possible movements.
