Recent research, based on the newly unearthed original source code from MIT, reveals that ELIZA, the pioneering chatbot developed in the mid-1960s, was more complex than previously understood. Contrary to its popular image as solely a 'therapist' program, the analysis shows ELIZA was capable of assuming multiple distinct personas. This discovery matters because it provides a deeper, more accurate understanding of early artificial intelligence development and the foundational principles of human-computer interaction. It challenges the long-held perception of ELIZA as a relatively simple program, highlighting the sophistication of its design for its era. The revelation underscores the ingenuity of its creator, Joseph Weizenbaum, and offers new insights into how early AI programs were structured to simulate conversation and evoke human-like responses, contributing to the phenomenon known as the 'ELIZA effect,' where users attribute human understanding to a machine. ELIZA was one of the first programs to simulate conversation, operating by pattern matching and rephrasing user input to give the impression of understanding. Its impact was profound, demonstrating how even a rudimentary AI could elicit emotional responses and engagement from users. This early experiment laid crucial groundwork for modern chatbots, natural language processing, and the ongoing exploration of human-AI interaction.
