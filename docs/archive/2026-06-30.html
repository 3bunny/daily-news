Read more
This research investigates the impact of assigning 'personality traits' to Large Language Models (LLMs) within multi-agent teams on their objective task performance. While personality prompting is known to alter LLM communication styles (e.g., low agreeableness leading to adversarial language), its effect on actual task outcomes has been less clear. The study manipulated personality traits across frontier LLMs in three task domains: structured coding, open-ended research collaboration, and competitive bargaining. It found that the influence of personality composition is highly dependent on the task structure. This finding is significant for designing and optimizing multi-agent AI systems. For highly structured tasks like coding, personality prompting had minimal impact on performance, despite changing communication. However, for complex, interactive tasks such as open-ended collaboration or bargaining, manipulating personality traits (e.g., low agreeableness) substantially degraded team performance. This suggests that for tasks requiring nuanced interaction, negotiation, or creative problem-solving, the 'personality' or behavioral disposition of AI agents can be a critical factor in achieving desired outcomes, offering crucial insights for future multi-agent system development.
