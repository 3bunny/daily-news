This paper introduces Few-Shot Domain Incremental Learning (FSDIL), a novel challenge in AI where models must adapt to new data domains with extremely limited new data, preventing overfitting. Traditional incremental learning often requires substantial data, making FSDIL critical for real-world scenarios with data scarcity. The authors propose the Continual Vision-Language Consolidation (CVLC) algorithm to address FSDIL. CVLC employs a technique called latent space reservation in the base domain, combined with dual coalescent projection (DCP) for efficient fine-tuning. It calibrates vision prototypes and generates language prototypes using Large Language Models (LLMs), then fuses these representations. This approach enables AI models to become more flexible and deployable in dynamic, data-constrained environments, such as adapting to new product categories or rare medical conditions with minimal new examples.

Read more
This paper introduces VirtueMap, a framework for profiling Large Language Models (LLMs) through an Aristotelian virtue-ethics lens. Instead of seeking a single 'correct' answer to ethical dilemmas, VirtueMap assesses how LLMs prioritize different virtues like Practical Wisdom, Justice, Truthfulness, Courage, and Temperance. It presents seven non-lethal, non-political, non-religious ethical dilemmas, each with five possible responses. Human respondents first rank these responses based on how well they express specific virtues, establishing a consensus-based reference order. LLMs then rank these responses, and their outputs are scored against the human-validated orderings using normalized Borda alignment, yielding a unique virtue profile for each LLM. This framework offers a nuanced way to understand an AI's ethical tendencies beyond simple right/wrong judgments, which is crucial for responsible deployment in sensitive applications and aligning AI behavior with human values.

This paper develops an economic model analyzing how firms manage human workers when deploying autonomous AI, considering that AI may fail and require human fallback. The model explores how firms decide on worker engagement, which impacts current output and future human skill through learning or erosion. It distinguishes between AI's 'capability' (output when working) and 'reliability' (probability of working). In a single-firm scenario, worker engagement is primarily an investment in fallback, with firms engaging less-skilled workers more to close skill gaps.
